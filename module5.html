Module 5: Heredity

Life continues through reproduction and heredity. This module explores how genetic information is passed on, expressed, and studied.

Welcome! You’ve taken the first step to study this module — that’s not always easy, so give yourself credit. Biology has a lot of content, but take a seat and let me break it down for you.

The first thing we’ll look at are the learning outcomes. It's just a roadmap, and the points sound way more complicated than they really are. But, they tell you exactly what skills and knowledge you’re meant to walk away with.

Keep them in mind as you go, because they’re what the examiners are looking for.

Learning Outcomes

1. “selects and processes appropriate qualitative and quantitative data and information using a range of appropriate media”

You’ll use numbers and observations and present them clearly using graphs tables, or diagrams.

2. “analyses and evaluates primary and secondary data and information”

You’ll practice deciding how trustworthy evidence is, comparing information from different sources.

3. “solves scientific problems using primary and secondary data, critical thinking skills and scientific processes”

You’ll use data and reasoning to solve science problems, just like real scientists do.

4. “explains the structures of DNA and analyses the mechanisms of inheritance and how processes of reproduction ensure continuity of species”

You’ll understand what makes up DNA and see how types of reproduction passes it on to keep species' going.

Content Focus

Life continues through the processes of reproduction and heredity. Students expand their knowledge of evolution by understanding the cellular processes involved in increasing genetic diversity. They investigate reproduction and inheritance patterns in both plants and animals as well as the role of DNA in polypeptide synthesis and the uses of technologies in the study of inheritance patterns.

Students also learn about contemporary research and the work of geneticists across a variety of industries, including medical applications and agriculture. They explore the effects on society and the environment through the application of genetic research.

What this means for you: This module is about understanding how life keeps going. You’ll look at how plants, animals, and even bacteria reproduce, how DNA builds proteins, and how scientists use genetics in medicine and farming. You’ll also think about the impact of all this on people and the environment.

Reproduction

Inquiry Question: How does reproduction ensure the continuity of a species?

Why reproduction matters

Reproduction passes on an organism’s genetic information to the next generation so species persist. Two main strategies exist: asexual and sexual.

Asexual reproduction — quick & identical

One parent produces offspring that are genetically the same. Fast and efficient, but no genetic variation.

  • Plants: runners (strawberry), tubers (potato).
  • Fungi: budding (yeast) or spores (mushrooms).
  • Bacteria: binary fission (E. coli)—one cell splits into two identical cells.
  • Protists: amoeba — binary fission.

Sexual reproduction — variation that helps survival

Two parents produce gametes (sperm + egg). Fertilisation mixes genes, producing variation that helps populations adapt.

External vs internal fertilisation

  • External: (e.g., frogs, many fish) eggs + sperm released into water — many offspring, low survival per egg.
  • Internal: (e.g., mammals, birds, reptiles) fertilisation inside the body — fewer offspring, higher survival/protection.

Plants — pollination to seed

Pollination (wind, insects, animals) transfers pollen to ovules. Fertilised ovules become seeds that grow into new plants (e.g., bee-pollinated apple trees).

Mammalian reproduction (short, clear)

After fertilisation the zygote becomes an embryo and implants into the uterus. Hormones (see glossary) control pregnancy and trigger birth when development is complete.

Applications in agriculture (practical examples)

  • Selective breeding: dairy cows bred for higher milk yield; wheat bred for disease resistance.
  • Artificial insemination: widely used in cattle to spread desirable genes without moving animals.
  • Controlled pollination / orchard management: hand pollination, timing and pollinator management to increase fruit set.
  • Micropropagation / cloning: tissue culture to produce identical crop plants (bananas, orchids).

Short tip: remember the trade-off — asexual = speed, sexual = variation. Think which strategy helps a species survive in a stable vs. changing environment.

Quick Glossary

Gametes
Sex cells: sperm (male) and egg (female).
Zygote
The first cell formed when sperm and egg fuse.
Uterus
The mammal organ where embryos develop.
Binary fission
Simple cell division in bacteria: 1 → 2 identical cells.
Pollination
Transfer of pollen from the anther to the stigma (leads to fertilisation in plants).
Progesterone
A hormone that helps maintain the uterine lining during pregnancy.
Oestrogen
A hormone that regulates female reproductive cycles and supports pregnancy.

Cell Replication

Inquiry Question: How important is it for genetic material to be replicated exactly?
  • Model mitosis and meiosis
  • Model DNA replication using the Watson and Crick model, showing nucleotide composition, pairing and bonding
  • Assess the effects of replication on species continuity

DNA and Polypeptide Synthesis

Inquiry Question: Why is polypeptide synthesis important?
  • Compare DNA forms in eukaryotes and prokaryotes
  • Model transcription and translation, analysing roles of mRNA and tRNA
  • Explain importance of polypeptide synthesis and gene–environment interactions in phenotypes
  • Investigate the structure and function of proteins in living things

Genetic Variation

Inquiry Question: How can the genetic similarities and differences within and between species be compared?
  • Model variation in offspring through meiosis, crossing over, fertilisation and mutation
  • Interpret inheritance patterns:
    • Autosomal traits
    • Sex-linkage
    • Co-dominance
    • Incomplete dominance
    • Multiple alleles
  • Use pedigrees and Punnett squares to model inheritance
  • Collect and analyse population frequency data, including SNP analysis

Inheritance Patterns in a Population

Inquiry Question: Can population genetic patterns be predicted with any accuracy?
  • Investigate use of technologies such as DNA sequencing and profiling to determine inheritance patterns
  • Analyse large-scale collaborative data to identify trends, e.g.:
    • Population genetics in conservation
    • Inheritance of diseases or disorders
    • Human evolution studies
